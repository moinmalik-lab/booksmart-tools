FUTA & SUTA 2026: The Complete Unemployment Tax Guide for Employers

What Are FUTA and SUTA?

FUTA (Federal Unemployment Tax Act) and SUTA (State Unemployment Tax Act) are employer-paid payroll taxes that fund unemployment insurance programs. When an employee loses their job through no fault of their own, these funds pay the unemployment benefits they receive.

Unlike FICA taxes, employees do not pay FUTA or SUTA — these are 100% employer obligations (with narrow exceptions in Alaska, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania where employees contribute a small amount to SUTA).

2026 Key Numbers: FUTA rate: 6.0% gross / 0.6% effective (with state credit) on first $7,000 per employee. Social Security wage base: $184,500. Most states use $7,000–$78,200 SUTA wage bases. California 2026: still at federal minimum $7,000 but subject to FUTA credit reduction.

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) — 2026 Details

FUTA is a federal employer tax that funds the administrative costs of the unemployment insurance system and provides emergency unemployment loans to states. Here are the 2026 specifics:

  • Gross FUTA rate: 6.0%
  • Taxable wage base: First $7,000 of each employee's wages per year (unchanged since 1983)
  • Maximum gross FUTA per employee: $420 ($7,000 × 6.0%)
  • State credit: Up to 5.4% if employer pays SUTA on time in a non-credit-reduction state
  • Net FUTA after credit: 0.6% × $7,000 = $42 per employee in most states

FUTA Credit Reduction States in 2026

When a state borrows from the federal unemployment trust fund to pay benefits and fails to repay the loans by November 10 two consecutive years, employers in that state lose part of their 5.4% FUTA credit. This raises the effective FUTA rate above 0.6%.

FUTA Deposits and Filing

FUTA is reported on Form 940 annually (due January 31). Deposits are required quarterly if FUTA liability exceeds $500 in a quarter. Most small employers with few employees can simply pay with the Form 940 filing. All FUTA deposits must be made via EFTPS.

State Unemployment Tax (SUTA) — How It Works

SUTA (also called SUI — State Unemployment Insurance) is the state-level companion to FUTA. Each state sets its own wage base, rate range, and new employer rate. The tax funds the actual unemployment benefits paid to workers in that state.

Your specific SUTA rate is assigned by the state based on your experience rating — essentially, your history of unemployment claims. New employers get a standard rate for the first two to three years, then transition to an experience-rated rate based on how many former employees filed for unemployment benefits.

Experience Rating: How Your SUTA Rate Is Set

Your SUTA rate is not arbitrary — it reflects your company's unemployment insurance history. The state maintains a reserve account for each employer. You contribute to it through SUTA payments; when your former employees claim unemployment, the state pays benefits from this account.

States use one of several methods to calculate experience ratings:

  • Reserve ratio method (most common): Compares your cumulative contributions minus benefits charged, divided by your average taxable payroll. A high reserve ratio = low rate.
  • Benefit ratio method: Divides total benefits charged to your account by your average taxable payroll.
  • Payroll decline method: Used in a few states; rates based on changes in your payroll.

Each fall, your state mails a SUTA rate notice for the upcoming year. Review it carefully — mistakes do occur, and you generally have a limited window to protest an incorrect rate.

Reducing your SUTA rate: The best long-term strategy is sound hiring and employee relations — fewer terminations means fewer claims. Some states also allow voluntary contributions to your reserve account to improve your reserve ratio and lower next year's rate. The ROI on voluntary contributions can be significant if it drops you to a lower rate bracket.

New Employer Rates

If you're a new employer, you'll be assigned a standard "new employer" rate while the state establishes your experience history. These rates vary widely:

  • California: 3.4% (construction: 9.1%)
  • Texas: 2.7%
  • Florida: 2.7%
  • New York: 4.025%
  • Illinois: 3.525%
  • Pennsylvania: 3.68%
  • Ohio: 2.7%

Most states also assign higher new employer rates for construction companies (which have higher layoff rates) than for other industries.

SUTA Dumping: What Not to Do

SUTA dumping is the illegal practice of transferring employees or payroll to a newly formed company to obtain a lower new-employer SUTA rate, avoiding the experience-rated rate earned by the original company. Federal law (the SUTA Dumping Prevention Act of 2004) requires all states to enact anti-dumping rules with penalties including criminal prosecution.

Legitimate business reorganizations, mergers, or acquisitions may require SUTA rate transfers — but these must be done properly with full disclosure to the state unemployment agency. When in doubt, consult a payroll compliance professional.

Multi-State SUTA: Which State Gets the Tax?

When an employee works in multiple states, you pay SUTA to the state where most of the work is performed (or the state of the employee's residence if they work in several states somewhat equally). The IRS and DOL use a four-factor test to determine the correct state:

  1. Where the service is localized (primarily performed in one state)
  2. The base of operations from which service is performed
  3. The place of direction and control
  4. The employee's state of residence

Remote workers have made this analysis significantly more complex. An employee who moved from California to Texas during the year may require a mid-year SUTA change between states.

How FUTA and SUTA Work Together

The relationship between FUTA and SUTA is intentional by design. The federal government provides a 5.4% credit against the 6.0% FUTA rate specifically to incentivize employers to pay into their state's unemployment system. This means:

  • If you pay SUTA on time: effective FUTA is 0.6% (you keep 5.4% as a credit)
  • If your state has a credit reduction: FUTA rises by the credit reduction percentage
  • If you're exempt from SUTA (e.g., 501(c)(3) nonprofits): you're also generally exempt from FUTA

The $7,000 FUTA wage base has not changed since 1983 despite decades of inflation. There are periodic legislative proposals to raise it, but none have passed. Many states have adopted much higher wage bases to maintain fund solvency.
