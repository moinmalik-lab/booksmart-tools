What Is FICA Tax?

FICA — the Federal Insurance Contributions Act — is the law that requires employers and employees to each contribute to two federal programs: Social Security and Medicare. Unlike federal income tax, which varies based on filing status and deductions, FICA rates are flat percentages of wages with no withholding elections possible.

Every employee and employer in the U.S. pays FICA taxes on covered wages. Self-employed individuals pay both sides through the Self-Employment (SECA) tax of 15.3%.

2026 Key Numbers: Social Security wage base: $184,500 (up from $176,100 in 2025). Combined FICA rate: 15.3% split equally — 7.65% each for employer and employee.

Social Security Tax: The $184,500 Wage Base

Social Security tax funds the federal retirement and disability insurance programs. In 2026, both the employee and employer each pay 6.2% on the first $184,500 of the employee's wages — a $8,400 increase from the 2025 base of $176,100.

Once an employee's cumulative wages for the year reach $184,500, Social Security withholding stops entirely for the remainder of the calendar year. The maximum Social Security tax each side pays in 2026 is $11,439 (6.2% × $184,500).

Employers must track cumulative wages carefully. If an employee works for two employers simultaneously, each employer independently applies the wage base — the employee may over-pay Social Security and claim a credit on their tax return, but employers have no obligation to coordinate.

Why Does the Wage Base Change Every Year?

The SSA adjusts the wage base annually based on changes in the national average wage index (NAWI). When wages across the economy rise, the wage base increases proportionally. This ensures Social Security benefits (which are also tied to average wages) remain funded. For 2026, the 4.8% increase reflects strong wage growth through mid-2025.

📊Example: High Earner Hits the Wage Base Mid-Year

Marcus earns $200,000 annually paid on a semi-monthly basis ($8,333.33 per paycheck). His employer deducts 6.2% ($516.67) for Social Security on each check.

After 22 paychecks (cumulative wages: $183,333), the 23rd paycheck ($8,333.33 in wages) would push total wages to $191,667 — over the $184,500 limit. Only the first $1,167 of that 23rd paycheck is subject to Social Security tax (6.2% × $1,167 = $72.35). After paycheck 23, Social Security withholding stops.

Maximum Social Security tax per employee in 2026: $11,439 employee | $11,439 employer | $22,878 combined

Medicare Tax: No Wage Cap

Medicare tax funds the federal health insurance program for people 65 and older (and qualifying younger people with disabilities). The rate is 1.45% each for employer and employee, with no wage cap — every dollar of wages is subject to Medicare tax.

Unlike Social Security, Medicare tax never stops during the year regardless of how much the employee earns. An executive earning $5 million pays 1.45% Medicare tax on all $5 million.

Additional Medicare Tax: 0.9% Over $200,000

The Affordable Care Act added an Additional Medicare Tax of 0.9% on employee wages exceeding $200,000 in a calendar year. This is an employee-only tax — there is no employer match for the additional Medicare tax.

Employers must begin withholding the additional 0.9% in the pay period when cumulative wages to that employee exceed $200,000 and continue for the rest of the calendar year. The employer is required to withhold this even if the employee's household income (combined with a spouse) would not actually trigger the tax — the employee reconciles on their return.

Key distinction: Employer withholding is triggered at $200,000 per employee (regardless of filing status). The actual tax liability is calculated on the employee's return using the household thresholds above. If the employer over-withholds (e.g., a married couple where only one spouse hits $200,000 but joint income is under $250,000), the employee gets a credit on their return.

How to Calculate FICA Each Pay Period

🧮FICA Calculation: Bi-weekly Payroll

Employee: Jennifer. Bi-weekly gross wages: $4,500. YTD wages through this payroll (before this check): $180,000.

Social Security: Remaining SS base = $184,500 − $180,000 = $4,500. Full paycheck is under the remaining base, so: $4,500 × 6.2% = $279.00

Medicare: $4,500 × 1.45% = $65.25

Additional Medicare: YTD after this check = $184,500. Under $200,000 threshold. $0

Two paychecks later (YTD: $193,500 and then $198,000): additional Medicare kicks in at $200,000 threshold.

Employee FICA this pay period: $344.25 | Employer match: $344.25 | Total FICA deposit: $688.50

FICA for Self-Employed: SECA Tax

Self-employed individuals pay both the employee and employer portions of FICA through the Self-Employment Tax (SECA). The rate is 15.3% on net self-employment income — 12.4% for Social Security (on the first $184,500) and 2.9% for Medicare (on all income).

However, self-employed individuals calculate SE tax on 92.35% of net earnings (not 100%) to account for the fact that employees only pay FICA on wages while employers also contribute. This reduces the effective SE tax base slightly.

Self-employed individuals can deduct half of the SE tax as an above-the-line deduction on Schedule 1 of Form 1040. This deduction reduces federal income tax (and adjusted gross income) but not SE tax itself.

Wages Exempt from FICA

Most compensation is subject to FICA, but several categories are exempt:

  • Tips under $20/month: Individual tips totaling less than $20 in a calendar month from a single employer are not subject to FICA. Tips of $20 or more must be reported by the employee and are subject to FICA.
  • Student FICA exception: Students employed by the school they attend may be exempt from FICA while enrolled at least half-time.
  • Railroad workers: Covered by the Railroad Retirement Tax Act (RRTA) instead of FICA.
  • Certain government employees: State and local government employees in some states participate in alternative retirement systems and may be exempt from Social Security (but not Medicare since 1986).
  • Religious order members: Members who take vows of poverty may be exempt.
  • Nonresident alien students: F-1, J-1, M-1 visa holders for the first five calendar years may be exempt.

FICA Reporting and Deposits

FICA taxes — both employee and employer portions — are deposited together with federal income tax withholding using the same monthly or semi-weekly schedule. All three components (income tax withheld, employee FICA, employer FICA) flow into the same EFTPS deposit and are reported on Form 941 quarterly.

At year-end, Social Security wages and Social Security tax withheld appear in W-2 Boxes 3 and 4; Medicare wages and Medicare tax withheld appear in Boxes 5 and 6.
