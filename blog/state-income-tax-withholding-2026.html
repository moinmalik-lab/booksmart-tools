State Income Tax Withholding: The Basics

In addition to federal income tax withholding, employers must withhold state income taxes for most employees in 41 states plus Washington D.C. Nine states — Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming — impose no personal income tax on wages.

State withholding is governed by each state's revenue department. Unlike federal withholding (one standard system), every state has its own withholding forms, tables, calculation methods, and deposit schedules. Managing withholding across multiple states is one of the most complex challenges in payroll compliance.

Major 2026 news: Eight states cut income tax rates effective January 1, 2026 — Indiana, Kentucky, Mississippi, Montana, Nebraska, North Carolina, Ohio, and Oklahoma. Ohio moved to a flat rate. Iowa completed its transition to a flat 3.8%. If you have employees in any of these states, your withholding tables need to be updated.

Oklahoma — Bracket Simplification

Oklahoma consolidated its six tax brackets into three for 2026, with a reduced top rate of 4.5% (down from 4.75%). Employers in Oklahoma should update withholding tables as the bracket thresholds changed significantly.

Georgia — Rate Phase-Down Continues

Georgia's flat income tax rate stands at 5.09% in 2026, continuing a legislated phase-down from 5.49%. The rate is scheduled to decrease further in subsequent years. Georgia has discussed (but not yet enacted) complete elimination of the income tax.

States With No Income Tax on Wages

These nine states impose no personal income tax, which means no state income tax withholding:

  • Alaska — no income tax (but does require SUTA employee contributions)
  • Florida — no income tax on wages
  • Nevada — no income tax
  • New Hampshire — taxes only interest and dividend income, not wages
  • South Dakota — no income tax
  • Tennessee — eliminated income tax on investment income in 2021; no wage tax
  • Texas — no income tax (but has significant property and franchise taxes)
  • Washington — no income tax on wages (has a capital gains tax; also the WA Cares long-term care payroll tax of 0.58%)
  • Wyoming — no income tax

Flat Rate vs. Graduated Rate States

States use one of two approaches to income taxation:

Flat Rate States (single rate for all income levels)

As of 2026, 15 states use a flat rate: Arizona (2.5%), Colorado (4.4%), Georgia (5.09%), Idaho (5.8%), Illinois (4.95%), Indiana (2.95%), Iowa (3.8%), Kentucky (3.5%), Massachusetts (5%), Michigan (4.25%), Mississippi (4.7%), North Carolina (3.99%), Pennsylvania (3.07%), Utah (4.55%), and Ohio (2.75% above $26,050).

Flat rate states are simpler to administer — one calculation, no bracket lookups. However, employees in flat rate states often find their withholding is less accurate than graduated states because flat-rate withholding doesn't account for the fact that lower-income employees face a lower marginal rate on their first dollars of income.

Graduated Rate States

Most states with income taxes use graduated brackets similar to the federal system. California has the most brackets and the highest top rate (13.3%), while other high-tax graduated states include New Jersey (10.75% top), New York (10.9% top), and Minnesota (9.85% top).

Local Income Taxes

Beyond state taxes, several major cities and counties impose their own income taxes that require employer withholding. This layer of complexity trips up many multi-location employers.

  • New York City: 3.078%–3.876% on top of the 10.9% state rate
  • Philadelphia: 3.75% for residents, 3.44% for non-residents working in Philadelphia
  • Columbus, Cleveland, Cincinnati (Ohio): Various local rates; many Ohio municipalities have income taxes
  • Detroit: 2.4% for residents, 1.2% for non-residents
  • Maryland counties: All 23 counties plus Baltimore City impose county income taxes (1.75%–3.2%) on top of the state rate
  • Kentucky cities: Dozens of cities impose local occupational license fees/taxes
  • Indiana counties: All 92 Indiana counties impose county adjusted gross income tax

State Withholding Forms (Equivalents to W-4)

Most states have their own withholding exemption certificate (the state equivalent of the federal W-4). Some states accept the federal W-4; others require their own form. Key points:

  • States that accept the federal W-4: Most states will accept it as a fallback if no state form is submitted
  • States with their own form: California (DE-4), New York (IT-2104), Arizona (A-4), Virginia (VA-4), Pennsylvania (REV-419), New Jersey (NJ-W4), and others
  • States with no form: A few states (like Pennsylvania with its flat 3.07% rate) have no employee withholding form because the calculation is always the same

Remote Workers: Which State's Tax Applies?

The rise of remote work has created significant complexity in state income tax withholding. The general rule is: withhold for the state where the employee performs the work, not where the employer is headquartered. But several nuances apply:

  • Reciprocity agreements: Many neighboring states have reciprocal agreements allowing employees to pay tax only in their home state, even if working in a different state. Examples: Virginia/Maryland/D.C./Pennsylvania/West Virginia all participate in a reciprocity network.
  • Convenience rule: New York, Delaware, Nebraska, Pennsylvania, and a few others use a "convenience of the employer" doctrine. Remote work done for a New York employer is taxable by New York unless the employee works remotely out of necessity for the employer (not their own convenience).
  • 30-day safe harbor: Several states (including Alabama starting 2026) provide a safe harbor — employers don't need to withhold until a non-resident employee has worked in the state for more than 30 days in a year.
Multi-state withholding tip: If an employee works in two states during the year, you generally need to withhold for both states based on wages earned in each. Most payroll software handles this automatically, but you need to track the state-apportioned wages accurately. Consult a payroll professional if you have employees working in high-tax states like New York or California.

State Tax Deposit Schedules

State withholding deposit schedules vary by state and often by employer size. Unlike the federal system (monthly or semi-weekly), states may require:

  • Quarterly: Small employers (common threshold: under $1,000–$2,500/year in withholding)
  • Monthly: Mid-size employers
  • Semi-monthly or bi-weekly: Larger employers
  • Accelerated/same-day: Very large employers (varies by state)

Many states also require electronic filing once you exceed a certain liability threshold. Check each state's Department of Revenue or Department of Taxation website for the current requirements and due dates.
