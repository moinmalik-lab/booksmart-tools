Profit Margin vs Markup: What's the Difference & Why It Matters for Your Business

📅 Updated January 2026⏱ 8 min read📈 Business Finance
Home → Blog → Margin vs Markup Guide

Confusing profit margin and markup is one of the most common — and expensive — mistakes small business owners make. Use the wrong formula when setting prices, and you can end up making less than you think. Some businesses go bankrupt because they confused 50% markup with 50% margin.

This guide explains both concepts clearly, shows you the exact formulas, gives real examples, and includes a conversion table so you can move between the two instantly.

The big mistake: A 50% markup gives you a 33.3% margin — NOT a 50% margin. If you set prices expecting 50% margin but used markup math, you're leaving significant money on the table.

The Core Difference in One Sentence

Markup is based on your cost. Margin is based on your selling price. Same numbers, completely different percentages.

Profit Margin
Margin = (Price − Cost) ÷ Price × 100
Based on: SELLING PRICE
Used by: Accountants, financial analysts, investors
Markup
Markup = (Price − Cost) ÷ Cost × 100
Based on: COST
Used by: Retailers, wholesalers, manufacturers

Real Example: A $50 Product

You buy a product for $30 and sell it for $50. What's your markup? What's your margin?

Example: $30 cost → $50 selling price

Cost (what you pay)$30.00
Selling price (what customer pays)$50.00
Gross profit (price − cost)$20.00
Markup (profit ÷ cost)66.7%
Gross Margin (profit ÷ price)40.0%

See how different those numbers look? Same product, same profit — but 66.7% markup vs 40% margin. If you tell your investors you have a "40% margin," that's the gross margin. If you tell your buyer you offer "66.7% markup," that's based on cost. Neither is wrong — they just measure different things.

The Formulas You Need to Know

If you know Cost and want to find Selling Price:

Using markup: Selling Price = Cost × (1 + Markup%/100)

Using margin: Selling Price = Cost ÷ (1 − Margin%/100)

Critical difference: A 50% markup → Selling Price = Cost × 1.5. A 50% margin → Selling Price = Cost ÷ 0.5 = Cost × 2. A "50% margin" requires doubling your cost — much more than a 50% markup!

Convert Between Markup and Margin:

To convert markup to margin: Margin = Markup ÷ (1 + Markup)

To convert margin to markup: Markup = Margin ÷ (1 − Margin)

Which Should You Use for Pricing?

Both metrics have their place:

  • Use markup when: You're a retailer or wholesaler pricing products where cost is your main input. Manufacturers and distributors typically use markup to ensure they cover costs plus a margin.
  • Use margin when: You're analyzing business performance, comparing against industry benchmarks, or talking to investors. Gross margin % is the standard financial metric for profitability analysis.
  • Industry standards: Grocery = 25–35% gross margin. Software = 70–90%. Restaurant food cost = 28–35% (meaning 65–72% margin on food). Retail apparel = 45–65%.

Why Net Profit Margin is More Important Than Gross Margin

Gross margin only accounts for the cost of goods sold (COGS). It ignores rent, salaries, marketing, and all operating expenses. That's why businesses with 70% gross margins can still lose money.

The three margins you should track:

  1. Gross Margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue — how efficiently you produce your product
  2. Operating Margin = (Revenue − COGS − Operating Expenses) ÷ Revenue — how efficiently you run the business
  3. Net Profit Margin = Net Income ÷ Revenue — the bottom line after everything including taxes and interest
