Sales Tax in the United States: How It Works

Sales tax in the U.S. is not a federal tax — it is levied entirely by states and localities. There is no national VAT. Each of the 45 states with a sales tax (plus Washington D.C.) sets its own base rate, exemptions, and local surtax rules. Counties and cities often pile on top of the state rate, leading to hundreds of thousands of distinct tax jurisdictions nationwide.

For businesses, this complexity means you may need to collect sales tax at different rates depending not just on the state but on the city and county where your customer is located. A sale into Los Angeles has a different rate than a sale into Sacramento — even though both are in California.

2026 overview: No states raised their statewide sales tax rate in 2026. Combined average rates (state + local) range from 0% (no-tax states) to 9.55% (Tennessee and Louisiana). Illinois simplified its economic nexus rules; Arkansas removed its state food tax; Maine added digital streaming to taxable sales.

States With No Sales Tax

Five states have no statewide sales tax: Alaska, Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, and Oregon. However, Alaska allows local jurisdictions to impose sales taxes — about 100 Alaskan municipalities do, and Alaska adopted a statewide economic nexus threshold for remote sellers in 2025.

Food and Grocery Exemptions

The treatment of food is one of the most variable aspects of state sales tax law. States use three general approaches:

  • Fully exempt: Unprepared grocery food is exempt from all state sales tax. States: California (mostly), Florida, Georgia, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, Texas, Vermont, Virginia (reduced rate), and more.
  • Reduced rate: Some states charge a lower rate on food. Tennessee charges 4% on groceries (vs. 7% standard). Virginia charges 2.5% (vs. 4.3% standard).
  • Fully taxable: Some states tax groceries at the full rate. Mississippi, South Dakota, and historically Arkansas. Arkansas eliminated its state food tax in 2026, though local taxes on food may still apply.

Prepared Food — A Different Story

Even in states that exempt groceries, prepared food is almost always taxable. "Prepared food" generally means food that is sold in a heated state, sold with eating utensils, or intended for immediate consumption. A bag of raw chicken is groceries (likely exempt). A rotisserie chicken from the hot case is prepared food (taxable).

Clothing Exemptions

A handful of states exempt clothing from sales tax, which matters enormously for apparel retailers:

  • Full exemption: Minnesota (clothing and shoes), New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Vermont exempt most clothing
  • Per-item threshold: New York exempts individual clothing items under $110 per item; Massachusetts exempts clothing under $175 per item; Connecticut exempts clothing under $50 per item
  • Sales tax holidays: Many states hold back-to-school tax-free weekends in August when clothing (and sometimes computers/school supplies) are temporarily exempt

Digital Goods and SaaS: A Rapidly Evolving Area

The taxability of digital products — software, streaming services, downloaded apps, SaaS subscriptions, and digital downloads — varies widely and is changing rapidly as states update their laws to capture more of the digital economy.

  • Taxable in most states: Digital downloads (music, movies, books purchased to own), electronically delivered software (prewritten software), streaming video/audio subscriptions
  • Maine 2026: Added digital audiovisual and audio services to taxable sales
  • SaaS specifically: Approximately half of states with sales tax now tax SaaS (Software as a Service). Texas, New York, and Pennsylvania tax SaaS. California, Florida, and Virginia generally do not.
  • Custom software: Generally not taxable even in states that tax prewritten software, because custom software is considered a service
SaaS sellers: Your product taxability analysis needs to be done state by state. The same software subscription can be taxable in Texas (which broadly taxes data processing) and non-taxable in California (which doesn't tax most SaaS). Use a tax engine like TaxJar, Avalara, or TaxCloud to automate this.

Sales Tax vs. Use Tax

Use tax is the buyer-side counterpart to sales tax. When a taxable purchase is made without paying sales tax (from an out-of-state seller who didn't collect, or a tax-exempt purchase used in a taxable way), the buyer owes use tax at the same rate as the sales tax.

Use tax is notoriously underreported by individuals. Businesses, however, face use tax audits regularly. Common business use tax situations include: office supplies ordered from out-of-state vendors, equipment purchased for resale but used internally, and promotional items.

Sales Tax Holidays 2026

Many states hold annual sales tax holidays — periods when certain products are temporarily exempt or subject to reduced rates. Popular types:

  • Back-to-school holidays (August): Florida, Texas, Iowa, Ohio, Missouri, and many others. Clothing, school supplies, and sometimes computers are exempt for a weekend.
  • Energy-efficient appliance holidays: Several Southern states hold spring or fall holidays for Energy Star appliances
  • Emergency preparedness: Florida and Alabama hold hurricane preparedness holidays before hurricane season
  • Firearms/hunting: Several states hold hunting-season holidays
