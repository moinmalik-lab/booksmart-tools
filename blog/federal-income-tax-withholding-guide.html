Federal Income Tax Withholding 2026: The Complete Employer Guide

Everything employers need to know about withholding federal income tax in 2026 — W-4 processing, percentage method tables, supplemental wages, and the key changes from the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.

What Is Federal Income Tax Withholding?

Federal income tax withholding is the amount an employer deducts from an employee's paycheck on behalf of the IRS. Rather than requiring employees to write one large check to the IRS each April, the U.S. uses a pay-as-you-go system: employers collect estimated taxes throughout the year and remit them to the government regularly.

The legal authority sits in IRC § 3402. Employers who fail to withhold correctly can be held personally liable for the trust fund portion of unpaid taxes — a serious risk that makes accuracy non-negotiable.

The mechanics of how much to withhold are detailed in IRS Publication 15-T (Federal Income Tax Withholding Methods), updated each December for the following year. The 2026 edition was released in December 2025 and incorporates significant changes from the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21).

Key fact: Withholding is not a tax itself — it is a prepayment of the employee's annual income tax liability. Any over-withholding is refunded when the employee files their return; under-withholding results in a balance due (and potentially penalties).

2026 Changes: One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)

Signed into law on July 4, 2025, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21) made the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions permanent and introduced several new tax benefits that directly affect payroll withholding in 2026.

What Changed for Employers in 2026

  • TCJA tax rates made permanent. The seven brackets (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%) are now permanent law rather than temporary provisions set to expire.
  • Qualified tips deduction (new). Employees in tip-earning occupations can deduct up to $25,000 of qualified tips from gross income. Employers must use updated W-4s to account for this deduction in withholding calculations.
  • Qualified overtime deduction (new). Employees can deduct up to $12,500 of qualified overtime pay ($25,000 for married filing jointly). Applies to overtime paid after 2024 and before 2029.
  • SALT cap raised. The state and local tax deduction cap increased from $10,000 to $40,000, affecting itemized deduction elections on W-4 Step 4(b).
  • Supplemental withholding rate unchanged. Remains at 22% (37% flat for wages exceeding $1 million in a year).
Action required: Employees who receive tips or overtime should submit updated 2026 W-4 forms to benefit from the new deductions in their withholding. Employers must use an employee's updated W-4 if submitted. IRS Publication 15-T (2026) explains the updated worksheets.

Processing the W-4 Form

The Form W-4 is the employee's instruction to the employer about how much federal income tax to withhold. Employers must follow the most recently submitted W-4 on file for each employee.

2020 and Later W-4 (Current Design)

The redesigned W-4 (effective 2020) removed withholding allowances and replaced them with five steps:

  • Step 1: Name, address, SSN, and filing status (Single/MFS, MFJ, or Head of Household).
  • Step 2: Multiple jobs or spouse works — employee checks a box or uses the IRS estimator. When checked, the employer uses the "Step 2 Checkbox" withholding rate schedules, which produce higher withholding.
  • Step 3: Claim dependents — reduces withholding by the child tax credit amount.
  • Step 4: Other adjustments — (a) other income not subject to withholding, (b) deductions the employee expects to itemize, (c) additional withholding per period.
  • Step 5: Signature and date.

Pre-2020 W-4s Still on File

If an employee submitted a W-4 before 2020 and has not submitted a new one, treat it as if the Step 2 checkbox is not checked. Use the number of withholding allowances claimed multiplied by the 2026 allowance value to calculate the deduction amount before applying the rate tables.

New for tips and overtime (2026): Employees can now include expected qualified tips or overtime deductions on W-4 Step 4(b) using the 2026 Deductions Worksheet in Publication 15-T. This reduces withholding on those wages so employees see the tax benefit in each paycheck instead of waiting until filing.

The Percentage Method: How It Works

Most modern payroll systems use the Percentage Method from Publication 15-T rather than the older Wage Bracket Method tables. The Percentage Method has no income ceiling (unlike the wage bracket tables which cap out below $100,000/year) and handles all filing statuses and W-4 configurations.

The Basic Calculation Steps (Percentage Method)

  1. Determine the employee's taxable wages for the pay period.
  2. If using a pre-2020 W-4: multiply the allowances claimed by the 2026 per-period allowance value and subtract from taxable wages.
  3. If using a 2020 or later W-4: add any Step 4(a) income or subtract Step 4(b) deductions from the annualized wages as instructed in Worksheet 1 of Publication 15-T.
  4. Look up the adjusted annualized wages in the Percentage Method tables for the employee's filing status.
  5. Apply the bracket percentage, add the flat dollar amount, and divide by pay periods.
  6. Add any Step 4(c) additional withholding per period.

Supplemental Wages

Supplemental wages are compensation paid in addition to an employee's regular wages — bonuses, commissions, overtime, severance, vacation payouts, and similar items. The withholding rules differ from regular wages.

Optional Flat Rate: 22%

When supplemental wages are paid separately from regular wages (separate check or separately identified on the same check), employers may optionally withhold at a flat 22% regardless of the employee's W-4 filing status. This is by far the most common approach because it's simple and auditable.

Aggregate Method

Alternatively, employers can add the supplemental wage to the most recent regular wage payment, withhold on the combined amount using the regular tables, and subtract out the withholding already taken on the regular wages. This method tends to produce withholding that better matches the employee's actual tax rate.

Mandatory 37% Flat Rate

Once total supplemental wages paid to an individual exceed $1,000,000 in a calendar year, all additional supplemental wages are subject to mandatory 37% withholding — no exceptions, regardless of the employee's W-4.

Qualified Tips & Qualified Overtime — New in 2026

The OBBBA created two new above-the-line deductions that directly affect payroll withholding starting in 2026.

Qualified Tips Deduction

Employees working in occupations that customarily and regularly received tips on or before December 31, 2024 may deduct up to $25,000 of qualified tips from their gross income. Qualified tips are voluntary cash tips or charges from customers (including tip-sharing arrangements). Mandatory service charges are not qualified tips.

To reduce their withholding to reflect this deduction, employees should enter the expected deductible tip amount on W-4 Step 4(b) using the Deductions Worksheet. Employers who receive an updated W-4 must use it.

Qualified Overtime Deduction

For tax years 2025–2028, employees may deduct up to $12,500 of qualified overtime compensation ($25,000 for married filing jointly). This applies to overtime pay earned under the Fair Labor Standards Act — regular time-and-a-half or other overtime premiums. Double-time, shift differentials, or other premium pay that isn't legally required overtime generally does not qualify.

Employer obligation: You must identify and separately track qualified overtime compensation on payroll records. Publication 15-T provides updated withholding worksheets for these new deductions. The IRS released an updated W-4 for 2026 that employees can use to elect these deductions into their withholding calculations.

Deposit Schedules: Monthly vs. Semi-Weekly

After withholding federal income tax plus the employer's share of FICA, employers must deposit those funds with the IRS on a specific schedule. The schedule is based on the employer's lookback period — the total employment tax liability reported in the four quarters ending June 30 of the prior year.

Employers with annual employment tax liability under $2,500 may pay with their quarterly Form 941 filing instead of making separate deposits.

Step-by-Step Withholding Calculation Example

📋 Example: Monthly Payroll, 2020 W-4, MFJ, Step 2 Box Unchecked

Employee: Sarah, married filing jointly. Submitted 2020 W-4 with Step 2 box unchecked, claimed $500/month in deductions on Step 4(b), no Step 4(a) or 4(c) entries.

Gross wages this month: $7,500

Step 1: Annualize gross wages: $7,500 × 12 = $90,000

Step 2: Subtract Step 4(b) deductions: $500 × 12 = $6,000 annualized. $90,000 − $6,000 = $84,000 adjusted annual wages

Step 3: Look up $84,000 in the MFJ Standard Withholding tables. Falls in the 12% bracket for MFJ ($23,850–$96,950). Tax = $2,385 + 12% × ($84,000 − $23,850) = $2,385 + $7,218 = $9,603 annual federal withholding

Step 4: Divide by pay periods: $9,603 ÷ 12 = $800.25 per month

Monthly federal withholding: $800.25 | Annualized: $9,603 | Effective rate: ~10.7%

Common Withholding Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

1. Using the Wrong Table for the W-4 Version

Publication 15-T has separate tables for pre-2020 W-4s and 2020+ W-4s, and further separates Step 2 checkbox checked vs. unchecked. Using the wrong table is the single most common employer error and typically results in systematic under-withholding for the employee.

2. Not Updating Withholding After an Employee Submits a New W-4

Employers must implement a new W-4 by the first payroll after the employee submits it, or by the first payroll of the next calendar quarter — whichever is later. Many payroll systems require manual action to activate a new W-4.

3. Ignoring the $200,000 Additional Medicare Threshold

While this is technically FICA rather than income tax withholding, it's often miscategorized. Once an employee's wages exceed $200,000 in a calendar year, employers must withhold the additional 0.9% Medicare surtax — even if the employee is married and their combined household income would not trigger the tax on their return. The employee will reconcile on their tax return.

4. Treating Mandatory Service Charges as Tips

With the new 2026 qualified tips deduction, the distinction matters more than ever. Mandatory service charges (gratuities added automatically to the bill) are wages, not tips, and do not qualify for the $25,000 tip deduction. Misclassifying them creates liability for both the employer and employee.

5. Missing the New Overtime Deduction Tracking Requirement

Employers must separately identify and document qualified overtime compensation to support the new deduction. If payroll records don't distinguish FLSA overtime from other premium pay, you can't accurately process W-4 requests from employees who want to elect the deduction.

Reporting: Form 941 and Form W-2

Federal income tax withholding is reported quarterly on Form 941 (Employer's Quarterly Federal Tax Return). Employers report total wages paid, income tax withheld, FICA taxes, and credits. Form 941 is due by the last day of the month following each quarter (April 30, July 31, October 31, January 31).

At year-end, employers must furnish each employee a Form W-2 by January 31 of the following year. Box 2 shows federal income tax withheld. The W-2 must also be filed with the Social Security Administration (SSA) by January 31, not just distributed to employees.

Penalty reminder: Failure to deposit on time: 2%–15% penalty depending on how late. Failure to file Form 941: 5% per month up to 25%. Failure to furnish W-2 by January 31: $50–$290 per form. The Trust Fund Recovery Penalty can make responsible persons personally liable for 100% of the trust fund portion of unpaid taxes.
