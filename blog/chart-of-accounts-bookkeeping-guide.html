Chart of Accounts: The Foundation of Good Bookkeeping

Every financial report your business produces is only as good as your chart of accounts. This guide covers the five account types, numbering systems, sample COA for service and product businesses, when to use sub-accounts vs. classes, and how to clean up a bloated chart.

What Is a Chart of Accounts?

The chart of accounts (COA) is a numbered list of every financial category your business uses to record transactions. Think of it as a filing system: every dollar that flows into or out of your business gets filed into one of these categories. The accuracy of your financial reports depends entirely on whether transactions are filed in the right place.

Every major accounting software — QuickBooks Online, Xero, FreshBooks, Zoho Books — uses a chart of accounts as its organizational backbone. While the software generates a default chart based on your industry, most businesses need to customize it. Getting your COA right from the start prevents months of cleanup work later.

Why this matters: A poorly structured chart of accounts produces meaningless financial reports. You can have perfectly imported bank transactions and still not know if your business is profitable if the transactions are miscategorized. COA design is the highest-leverage bookkeeping decision you make.

The Five Account Types

Every account in your COA belongs to one of five types. Understanding these types is the foundation of double-entry bookkeeping:

TypeWhat It TracksNormal BalanceExamples
AssetsWhat you own or are owedDebit (increases with debits)Checking, savings, A/R, inventory, equipment
LiabilitiesWhat you owe to othersCredit (increases with credits)A/P, credit cards, loans, payroll liabilities
EquityOwner's stake in the businessCreditOwner equity, retained earnings, owner draws
IncomeRevenue earnedCreditProduct sales, service revenue, interest income
ExpensesCosts of running the businessDebitPayroll, rent, utilities, supplies, insurance

The fundamental accounting equation — Assets = Liabilities + Equity — must always hold true. Every transaction affects at least two accounts (one debit, one credit), which is why this is called "double-entry bookkeeping."

Account Numbering System

Most chart of accounts use a numbering system to organize accounts by type and allow easy expansion:

Number RangeAccount Type
1000–1999Assets
2000–2999Liabilities
3000–3999Equity
4000–4999Income / Revenue
5000–5999Cost of Goods Sold
6000–9999Operating Expenses

Leaving gaps in numbering (e.g., 6010, 6020, 6030 rather than 6001, 6002, 6003) lets you insert new accounts in logical places later without renumbering everything.

Chart of Accounts for Product Businesses

If you sell physical products, your COA needs additional accounts to properly track inventory and cost of goods sold:

  • 1300 — Inventory Asset: The value of goods you own and haven't sold yet. This is an asset account on the balance sheet.
  • 5010 — Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): The cost of inventory you sold this period. Appears between Income and Operating Expenses on the P&L. Critical for calculating gross profit.
  • 5020 — Purchase Returns and Allowances: Credits received from suppliers for returned goods.
  • 5030 — Freight-In: Shipping costs to receive inventory — these are part of the cost of goods, not an operating expense.
COGS vs. Operating Expenses: A common bookkeeping error is recording inventory purchases as an operating expense (like "Supplies"). This understates assets and misrepresents gross margin. Every dollar of purchased inventory should hit the Inventory Asset account first, then move to COGS when the item is sold.

Sub-Accounts: Adding Detail Without Complexity

Sub-accounts let you see both the total for a category and the breakdown within it. For example:

  • 6010 Payroll (parent account — shows total payroll cost)
    • 6011 — Regular Wages
    • 6012 — Overtime Wages
    • 6013 — Bonuses & Commissions
    • 6014 — Employer Payroll Taxes
    • 6015 — Employee Benefits

QBO, Xero, and most other platforms support parent/child account hierarchies. Reports can show the total (parent) or expanded view (all sub-accounts). Use sub-accounts strategically for categories where the breakdown genuinely matters for decisions — payroll components, different marketing channels, multiple service lines — but avoid creating sub-accounts just for the sake of granularity.

Classes and Locations: When Not to Use Accounts

A common mistake is creating separate accounts for every department, location, or product line. This bloats the COA and makes reports hard to read. Instead, use Classes and Locations (available in QBO Plus and above):

  • Classes — Track by business segment, department, product line, or owner in a partnership. One expense account (Payroll) can be split across multiple classes (Marketing, Operations, Admin).
  • Locations — Track by physical location or property. Useful for businesses with multiple stores, rental units, or project sites.

With Classes active, your P&L can show total company performance OR performance by class — without creating separate accounts for each segment. This is the right tool for segmentation, not the COA.

Cleaning Up a Messy Chart of Accounts

If you've inherited a COA that's grown organically and is now cluttered with duplicate accounts, vague categories, or thousands of rarely-used entries, here's the cleanup approach:

  1. Run a transaction count report by account. Accounts with zero or very few transactions are candidates for merger or deletion.
  2. Identify duplicates. Common examples: "Meals & Entertainment" and "Food and Beverage" both exist. Merge the smaller one into the larger one.
  3. Collapse miscellaneous buckets. If more than 10% of your expenses land in "Miscellaneous," that account is hiding information. Review those transactions and create proper accounts.
  4. Use the account merge function. In QBO, you can merge two accounts — all historical transactions reclassify automatically. There's no need to manually re-enter old transactions.
  5. Close unused accounts. QBO lets you make accounts inactive. Inactive accounts don't appear in dropdown menus but retain their history.
